# Example Digital Collections
## Large collections

1. **Digital Public Library of America — DPLA**  
   A large U.S. aggregator that brings together digitized materials from libraries, archives, museums, and other cultural heritage organizations. Useful for discussing metadata aggregation, normalization, search across heterogeneous collections, and national-scale digital library infrastructure.

2. **Europeana**  
   A major European cultural heritage aggregator, drawing from libraries, museums, archives, and audiovisual collections across Europe. Useful for discussing multilingual access, international aggregation models, metadata standards, rights statements, and cultural heritage policy.

3. **Gallica — Bibliothèque nationale de France**  
   The digital library of the Bibliothèque nationale de France, including books, newspapers, manuscripts, maps, images, sound recordings, and other materials. Useful for discussing national library digitization, faceted browsing, OCR, historical newspapers, and public access to large-scale collections.

4. **Library of Congress Digital Collections / World Digital Library**  
   A large set of digitized collections from the Library of Congress, including manuscripts, maps, photographs, newspapers, audio, film, and rare books. Useful for discussing collection-level organization, item description, digitization at national scale, and public-history presentation.

5. **Biodiversity Heritage Library — BHL**  
   A large thematic digital library focused on biodiversity literature, including books, journals, field diaries, scientific illustrations, and taxonomic literature. Useful for discussing domain-specific digital libraries, OCR, scientific reuse, open access, and sustainability.

6. **Smithsonian Libraries and Archives Digital Collections**  
   Digital collections from the Smithsonian Libraries and Archives, including books, manuscripts, photographs, trade literature, ephemera, seed catalogs, and art-related files. Useful for discussing museum-library hybrid collections, institutional collections, visual materials, and digitization priorities.

## Smaller thematic collections

1. **East Asia Image Collection — Lafayette College Libraries**  
   A focused image collection of photographs, negatives, postcards, and slides related to imperial Japan, its Asian empire, and occupied Japan. Useful for discussing visual collections, historical context, place metadata, colonial/imperial framing, and ethical description.

2. **World War I Diaries and Letters — University of Iowa Libraries**  
   A manuscript-centered collection of soldiers’ diaries and letters from World War I. Useful for discussing transcription, item-level description, personal archives, handwritten materials, and classroom use of primary sources.

3. **Prints, Drawings & Watercolors from the Anne S. K. Brown Military Collection — Brown University Library**  
   A focused visual-material collection drawn from Brown University’s military history holdings. Useful for discussing image metadata, subject access, collection scope, genre/form description, and legacy digital-library interfaces.

4. **Lafayette College Archives Image Collection — Lafayette College Libraries**  
   A campus-history image collection documenting buildings, people, events, and institutional history. Useful for discussing modest-scale institutional collections, local audiences, campus memory, basic image metadata, and rights/status statements.

5. **Civil Rights Digital Library — University of Georgia Libraries / Digital Library of Georgia**  
   A thematic collection focused on the history of the civil rights movement, including archival footage, documents, photographs, oral histories, and related educational resources. Useful for discussing thematic aggregation, public history, audiovisual access, civil rights documentation, and teaching-oriented digital collections.

6. **Women Who Rock Digital Oral History Archive — University of Washington Libraries**  
   A focused oral-history collection documenting women and popular music scenes, especially around community, activism, and cultural production. Useful for discussing oral history, audiovisual metadata, community-engaged archives, participant-centered description, and the presentation of interviews online.
